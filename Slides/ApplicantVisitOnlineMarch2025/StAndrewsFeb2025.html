Model development

Algorithm outline

Let \(N=N_{i,j,k}\) represent number of units on \(i^{th}\) day of age \(j\) and blood group \(k\).

Let \(n_i,k\) be the total number of units of type \(k\).

  • At each simulated day:

    • Identify the day of the week and the demand events that occurred on that day.

    • How many units of a certain blood type are needed?

    • Randomly sample from appropriate supply distribution

    • Repopulate ‘fridge’ with new units up to stock threshold, \(\mathbf{r}\).

    • Simulate each recorded transfusion, removing units using the preference matrix.

Optimisation

Let \(n_e\) represent the level of blood that triggers a ‘spare-capacity’ emergency restock. Define \[ E(\mathbf{r})=\sum_{k=1}^{8}\sum_{i=0}^{N_T}N_{i,j=N_A,k}+ \gamma \sum_{k=1}^{8}\sum_{i=0}^{N_T} \mathbf{1}(n_{i,k}<n_e), \] where \(\gamma\) represents the relative cost of one spare capacity event per wasted unit of blood.

We want to identify \(\min_{\mathbf{r}}(E(\mathbf{r}))\).

  • We applied a simulated annealing algorithm to identify the vector \(\mathbf{r}^*\) that minimises \(E(\mathbf{r^*})\).

  • The search space was constrained so that the ratio of thresholds was within a tolerance factor of background population frequencies.

Time averaging

What is going on?

  • Demand is oscillatory.

  • Friday is the biggest delivery day when units are 2 or 3 days old.

  • When supply model is changed to make age distribution of supplied units independent of day, the waves are much less pronounced.

Suggestion: a time-dependent stocking strategy

  • Wednesday - increase thresholds by 25% from base line

  • Friday - decrease thresholds by 25% from base line

  • Stocks averaged over the week are at the same levels

  • Reduce amount of units delivered on Friday

  • Dampens oscillatory dynamics

  • Emergency call outs reduce from 7 to 2.

A single population model (no cross grouping)

Independent variables: \(a\) and \(t\)

Dependent variable: \(q=q(a,t)\) number density of RBC units

Integro-PDE: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial {q}}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial {q}}{\partial a}= k_1(t)(r-n(t-\tau)){f}(a) -k_2(t) {q} {g}(a,a^*(t)), \quad (t,a) \in (0,T] \times (0,A]. \label{MainQeqnTime} \end{aligned} \]

Total number of RBC units: \[ \begin{aligned} n(t)=\int_{0}^A {q}(a,t)\mathrm{d}a. \end{aligned} \]

\(f=f(a)\): prescribed age distribution of supplied units

Demand term

\[ g(a,a^*(t))= \begin{cases} 1& a \geq a^*(t),\\ 0, & a < a^*(t), \end{cases} \]

where \(a^*(t)\) is defined by \[ \begin{aligned} \eta(t)=\int_{a^*(t)}^A {q} \mathrm{d}a. \end{aligned} \]

A boundary condition at age \(a=0\) is given by \[ \begin{aligned} {q}(0,t) \equiv 0, \nonumber \end{aligned} \] The initial conditions are: \[ \begin{aligned} {q}(a,0)=q_0(a), \quad a \in (0, A]. \label{IC} \end{aligned} \]

Two simplifying assumptions

Source term non zero: \[a\in[a_0,a_1]\] Sink term non zero: \[a\in[a^*,A]\]

where \[ a_0<a_1<a^*<A. \].

Approx. as uniform

A steady state solution

\[ \begin{aligned} q&=0, \quad 0<a<a_0, \nonumber \\ {q}&=\frac{k_1}{a_1-a_0}(r-n)(a-a_0), \quad a_0\leq a \leq a_1, \nonumber \\ {q}&= k_1(r-n), \quad a_1<a<a^*, \nonumber\\ {q}&= k_1(r-n)e^{-k_2(a-a^*)}, \quad a^*\leq a\leq A. \label{Steadystate3} \end{aligned} \] where \[ \begin{aligned} a^*=A+\frac{1}{k_2}\ln\left( 1-\frac{k_2\left(\frac{1}{k_1}+\gamma(a^*)\right)}{(\frac{r}{\eta}-1)} \right), \end{aligned} \]

Derivation of expressions for KPIs

The WAPI is:

\[ \begin{aligned} WAPI=100e^{-k_2(A-a^*)}. \end{aligned} \]

The ISI is: \[ \begin{aligned} ISI=\frac{\frac{1}{k_1}+\frac{r}{\eta}(a^*-\mu)}{\frac{r}{\eta}-1}. \end{aligned} \]

KPIs depend on \(a^*\) which is defined implicitly.

The low wastage limit

Assume \[ a^*\ll A \]

Hence \[ a^*=\frac{r-\eta}{k_2\eta}-\frac{1}{k_1}+\mu \] and \[ \begin{aligned} WAPI&=100e^{-k_2(A+\frac{1}{k_1}-\mu)}e^{\frac{r}{\eta}-1}, \nonumber\\ ISI&= \frac{r}{k_2\eta}-\frac{1}{k_1}=a_{exp}-\mu, \nonumber \\ a_{exp}&=\frac{r}{k_2\eta}-\frac{1}{k_1}+\mu. \label{derivedKPIs} \end{aligned} \]

Hence, explicit formula for KPIs.

Conclusions and future work

Conclusions

  • Develop a mechanistic mathematical model that could be used to explore red blood cell stocking strategy.

  • We did this and validated the model output by comparing predictions with measurements.

  • Optimisation indicated that PRI could stock more OPos without increasing waste,

  • The age distribution of supplied units results in peaks and troughs in the age distribution.

  • Dampening the oscillatory phenomenon by using time dependent thresholds reduced emergency call outs.

  • Next step is to validate predictions using a new dataset.
